About

Documentary journalism, independent

I make long-form audio documentaries — for the BBC, and independently. My work covers the stories that take time: American politics, landscape and place, historical consequence.

I've always been fascinated by James Cook — Captain Cook as we called him back then. It was a point of pride that, like me, he was born near Middlesbrough. On family days out we'd walk up to the Captain Cook monument on the Cleveland Hills, where it overlooks the Vale of York. For our early family holidays we went regularly to Staithes — picturesque fishing village on the North Sea coast where Cook learned to sail — and to nearby Whitby, the historic port where he was an apprentice, and where they built the ship that would take him to the other side of the world.

But for all that, Cook remained a romantic explorer of my childhood. I never imagined I'd get any closer to him than reading about his discoveries and studying maps of his voyage.

And yet in early 2026 I found myself — unexpectedly — on an island in the Pacific. Suva, on Fiji. And that gave me a first-hand opportunity to research the story of Cook's exploration of the Pacific — to find out more about where he went, what he did, and to understand the consequences of his three voyages for the Pacific today. That's the basis of this Cook in the Pacific podcast series.
